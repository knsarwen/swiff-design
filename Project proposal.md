Based on the uploaded proposal, this project is a **hyperlocal urban fulfilment platform** — basically an app that connects people or businesses who need something done **immediately nearby** with verified runners who can complete short-distance errands, deliveries, pickups, and small tasks within city areas.

## 1. Project concept

The project is called **Project **786637****, and the main idea is **Urban Hyperlocal Fulfillment**.

This means the platform focuses on very short-distance, fast-response tasks inside busy urban zones such as office areas, business districts, malls, neighborhoods, and dense city locations. Instead of using normal delivery services that may take longer or operate on wider routes, this platform focuses on **nearby runners who are already within the same micro-zone**.

The platform is designed for situations like:

Someone forgot to buy a drink.

Someone needs coffee picked up.

A staff member needs to send a document or item to another office nearby.

Someone needs to buy cigarettes, snacks, food, or small items.

A business needs quick point-to-point delivery.

A customer needs a small task done immediately by someone nearby.

So the project is not only a normal delivery app. It is more like an **urban assistant / personal shopper / instant runner service**.

## 2. What problem does it solve?

The proposal is targeting a very common urban problem: people often need small things done quickly, but they do not want to leave their office, meeting, home, shop, or current location.

For example, in a business district, someone may need:

a coffee pickup,

a document sent to another office,

lunch collected,

small items bought,

urgent delivery between offices,

or a quick errand completed nearby.

Current delivery platforms like Grab, Shopee, Lazada, and courier services are usually designed for food delivery, parcels, e-commerce, or larger logistics. This proposed platform is trying to occupy the gap between **personal errands** and **instant short-distance fulfilment**.

The main value proposition is:

**“I need someone nearby right now.”**

That is the core problem the app wants to solve.

## 3. How the platform works

The proposed flow is quite clear. It involves the customer, the platform, and the verified runner.

The process starts when a customer places a request through the mobile or web app. The request can be a point-to-point delivery, pickup, buying task, or small errand.

After the request is placed, the platform validates the request and proposes suitable runners. The matching algorithm considers things like:

distance,

item weight,

required time,

availability of runners,

and possibly the location zone.

Once a runner is matched, the platform holds the payment first. This is important because it protects both the customer and the runner. The customer knows payment will only be released after successful completion, while the runner knows the money is already secured.

The runner is notified and can accept the job. Once accepted, the timer starts. The runner then picks up or buys the item, pays if required, collects the item, and delivers it face-to-face.

After the customer receives the item, they confirm successful delivery. Once the transaction is confirmed, the platform closes the job and releases payment to the runner.

The proposed payout model gives **80% of the payment to the runner**.

## 4. Runner model

A key part of the proposal is that the runners are not meant to be random strangers. The slides highlight that the runners should be:

registered,

trained,

uniformed,

background checked,

verified,

and monitored.

This is very important because trust is one of the biggest concerns in a service like this. Since runners may be handling personal items, money, food, office documents, or small purchases, customers need to feel safe.

The proposal positions the runner as a **verified fulfilment agent**, not just a casual gig worker. That makes the platform more trustworthy compared to an open system where anyone can suddenly become a runner without checks.

## 5. Target cities and market size

The proposal targets major Malaysian urban and business locations, including:

Kuala Lumpur,

Petaling Jaya,

Subang,

Cyberjaya,

Putrajaya,

Penang,

Johor Bahru,

and Ipoh.

The estimated total population across the targeted areas is around **18.04 million**. The proposal then narrows this further by looking at employment and daytime activity, especially between **7 AM and 7 PM**.

The estimated daytime employment-based customer base is around **9.9 million people**.

The project focuses especially on places with:

dense office clusters,

heavy pedestrian movement,

high working population,

and familiarity with food delivery services.

This makes sense because the platform will work best in places where many people are close together and short-distance errands happen frequently.

## 6. Target users

The target users can be divided into several groups.

First, there are **office workers**. These are people working in business districts who may need food, coffee, documents, supplies, or small personal items without leaving their workplace.

Second, there are **businesses and SMEs**. A company may need to send small items between branches, collect something nearby, or perform quick office errands.

Third, there are **urban consumers**. These are normal users living or moving around city areas who need quick help nearby.

Fourth, there are **runners/gig workers**. These are people who want to earn income by completing simple, low-distance, low-complexity tasks.

## 7. Why the proposal believes this can work

The proposal gives several reasons why this platform could work in Malaysia.

Malaysia already has a strong convenience culture. People are used to paying for speed and convenience, especially in urban areas. The slides describe this as a “malas” culture, meaning people often prefer convenience when the cost is reasonable.

Malaysia also has high mobile app adoption. Many people are already comfortable using apps for food delivery, e-commerce, banking, transport, and payments.

Another reason is the strong delivery culture built by platforms like Grab, Lazada, and Shopee. Because users already understand app-based ordering and delivery tracking, this new platform does not need to educate the market from zero.

The proposal also says Malaysia is tolerant of gig work. This means people are already familiar with the idea of part-time or flexible work through platforms.

Another major point is digital payment adoption. Since users are already comfortable with online payments and e-wallets, the platform can use a payment-hold and release model more easily.

The project also argues that the tasks are low distance and low complexity. Because the work is simple, more people can become runners, and the platform may not need highly skilled workers.

## 8. Challenges identified in the proposal

The proposal also lists several possible challenges.

The first challenge is demand: **Will there be enough requests?** A platform like this only works if enough customers use it regularly.

The second challenge is supply: **Will there be enough runners?** If users place requests but no runner accepts quickly, the platform will fail.

Another challenge is trust. Customers may ask:

How can I trust the runner?

What if the item is missing?

What if the runner buys the wrong item?

What if the runner delays?

What if the runner disappears?

There are also policy issues, such as:

refund handling,

wrong item disputes,

missing item claims,

multiple-item pricing,

runner payment responsibility,

and customer cancellation.

The app also needs strong matching logic. If the app cannot match a runner quickly, the main promise of “nearby right now” becomes weak.

Marketing is another challenge because users may ask, “What app is this?” Since this is a new category, branding and awareness will be very important.

## 9. Proposed brand positioning

The project seems to position itself as more than a delivery app. The phrase used is:

**Urban Assistant @ Personal Shopper**

This means the app can be branded as a helper for city people — someone nearby who can assist with quick tasks, buy items, collect things, or deliver something immediately.

This positioning is useful because it separates the project from normal food delivery or courier services. It makes the platform feel more flexible and human.

## 10. Business model

From the slides, the clearest business model is transaction-based.

The customer pays a fee for the task. The payment is held by the platform first. After successful completion, the payment is released to the runner, with **80% going to the runner**. The remaining 20% is likely the platform’s revenue or commission, although the slide does not explicitly explain how that 20% is used.

There may also be future revenue opportunities such as:

business subscriptions,

priority matching,

corporate accounts,

delivery insurance,

bulk errands,

premium verified runners,

or zone-based business partnerships.

But these are not explicitly detailed in the slides, so they should be treated as possible extensions, not confirmed proposal items.

## 11. My interpretation of the full project

Overall, this project proposes a **real-time micro-errand and short-distance fulfilment platform** for Malaysian cities.

The main idea is simple:

A person needs something nearby.

They place a request.

The app finds a verified nearby runner.

Payment is held securely.

The runner completes the task.

Customer confirms success.

Payment is released.

The project depends heavily on **speed, trust, density, and liquidity**. “Liquidity” here means having enough customers and enough runners in the same area at the same time. If the platform launches in too many areas too early, it may struggle. But if it starts in one dense business district, such as **KLCC**, Bangsar South, Cyberjaya, PJ, or George Town, it has a better chance of success.

The strongest part of the proposal is that it targets a real daily pain point: small urgent tasks that are too minor for formal courier services but still annoying for people to do themselves.

The weakest part is that the operational policies are not fully defined yet. The proposal identifies these issues, but it still needs stronger detail on refunds, wrong items, runner verification, cash handling, prohibited items, customer disputes, service fees, and runner incentives.

## 12. What is being proposed, in one complete explanation

The project proposes a mobile and web-based urban runner platform where customers can request quick nearby assistance for errands, deliveries, pickups, and small purchases. The system operates inside micro-zones in dense Malaysian cities and matches users with verified runners based on distance, time, item size or weight, and availability. Customers place a request, the system validates it, payment is held, a runner is notified, the runner accepts the job, completes the pickup or purchase, delivers the item face-to-face, and the customer confirms success. Once confirmed, the platform releases payment to the runner, with the proposed payout being 80% to the runner. The platform targets business cities and office-heavy areas such as KL, PJ, Subang, Cyberjaya, Putrajaya, Penang, JB, and Ipoh, with an estimated daytime employment-based customer base of about 9.9 million. The proposal believes the idea can work because Malaysia has high mobile app usage, strong delivery culture, digital payment adoption, acceptance of gig work, and high demand for convenience. However, the project must solve challenges around request volume, runner supply, trust, refunds, wrong items, missing items, pricing, runner matching, and brand awareness.

## 13. Important things still missing from the proposal

The current proposal is a good high-level concept, but it still needs more detail before development or investor presentation.

It should define the exact service categories, for example:

buy for me,

pickup and drop-off,

office-to-office delivery,

food or drink collection,

queue service,

document delivery,

and personal shopping.

It should also define prohibited items. For example, cigarettes were mentioned in the slide, but this may involve age restrictions, compliance, and legal concerns. The platform must be very careful with items such as tobacco, alcohol, medicine, cash, confidential documents, and expensive goods.

The proposal also needs a clear fee model. For example:

base fee,

distance fee,

waiting time fee,

multi-item fee,

urgent fee,

cancellation fee,

and refund policy.

It also needs a runner onboarding process, customer support flow, dispute resolution policy, insurance/liability rules, and operational **SOP**.

## 14. Final project summary

This is a **hyperlocal runner marketplace** for Malaysian urban areas. It connects people who need immediate nearby help with verified runners who can complete short-distance errands quickly. The main promise is speed, convenience, trust, and affordability. The project is strongest in dense office and commercial areas where people often need small tasks done during working hours. The idea has potential, but its success will depend on strong runner supply, clear policies, safe payment handling, trust-building, and focused city-by-city rollout.
